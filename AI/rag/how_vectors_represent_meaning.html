How Do Vectors Represent Meaning?

Category: AI → RAG

Introduction

When working with embeddings, one of the first questions people ask is:

How can a list of numbers represent the meaning of text?

At first glance, it seems impossible. How can something like:

[0.12, -0.45, 0.88, ...]

capture the meaning of a sentence, a document, or even a person's career?

The answer lies in how embedding models organize information in a mathematical space where similar meanings are placed close together.

Beyond Words and Sentences

Embeddings are not limited to individual words. Modern embedding models can represent:

  • Characters
  • Tokens
  • Words
  • Sentences
  • Paragraphs
  • Documents
  • Job descriptions
  • Products
  • User profiles
  • Career histories

Anything that contains information can potentially be represented as a vector. For example:

  • A document can be represented by a vector.
  • A product listing can be represented by a vector.
  • A person's professional experience can be represented by a vector.

The vector acts as a numerical summary of the meaning contained within that information.

Vectors as Points in Space

A vector can be thought of as a point in space. For a simple three-dimensional vector:

[2, 5, 8]

the numbers represent:

X = 2
Y = 5
Z = 8

This places the vector at a specific location in three-dimensional space.

Modern embedding models work the same way, except instead of three dimensions they may use:

  • 384 dimensions
  • 768 dimensions
  • 1536 dimensions
  • 3072 dimensions

Rather than a point in 3D space, an embedding becomes a point in a high-dimensional mathematical space.

Although humans cannot visualize thousands of dimensions, computers can efficiently calculate distances between points in these spaces.

Similar Meanings Live Close Together

The most important property of embeddings is:

Similar meanings produce vectors that are close together.

Consider the following questions:

What are the ticket prices from New York to London?

What is the price of a flight from JFK to Heathrow?

The wording is different. The exact words are different. However, the meaning is nearly identical.

An embedding model converts both sentences into vectors that occupy nearby positions in vector space.

Sentence A → Vector A
Sentence B → Vector B

Because the meanings are similar:

Vector A ≈ Vector B

This property allows computers to search and compare information based on meaning rather than exact keywords.

The Discovery That Changed NLP

One of the most important discoveries in Natural Language Processing came from a technique called Word2Vec.

Word2Vec was an early embedding system that converted individual words into vectors. Each word occupied a position within vector space. For example:

King
Queen
Prince
Emperor

would naturally appear close to one another because they share related meanings. Similarly:

Apple
Orange
Banana
Mango

would form another cluster representing fruit.

The surprising discovery was that these vectors captured relationships between concepts.

Vector Arithmetic

Researchers found that mathematical operations on vectors often produced meaningful results. A famous example is:

King - Man + Woman = Queen

Conceptually:

  1. Start with the meaning of "King".
  2. Remove the concept of "Man".
  3. Add the concept of "Woman".

The resulting vector ends up near the vector representing "Queen".

This was remarkable because nobody explicitly programmed this rule into the system. The relationships emerged naturally during training.

Another Example

The same idea can be applied to locations.

Paris - France + England = London

The embedding captures relationships between:

  • Capitals
  • Countries
  • Geographic concepts

Again, the result emerges from how meanings are organized within vector space.

Meaning Instead of Words

The most important insight is that embeddings do not primarily represent words. They represent meaning.

Consider these two sentences:

I want to buy a new laptop.

I need a computer for work.

The wording differs significantly. However, both sentences express related intentions.

An embedding model focuses less on the exact words and more on the underlying concepts. As a result, their vectors will be closer together than completely unrelated sentences.

Modern Embeddings

Word2Vec worked with individual words. Modern embedding models are far more powerful. They can generate embeddings for:

  • Sentences
  • Paragraphs
  • Entire documents

For example:

A product description       → a single vector
A research paper            → a single vector
A customer support chat     → a single vector

This allows modern AI systems to compare and retrieve information at much larger scales.

Measuring Similarity

When comparing vectors, we need a way to determine how similar they are. The most common technique is called Cosine Similarity.

Cosine similarity measures how closely two vectors point in the same direction.

Higher similarity means:

  • Similar meanings
  • Related concepts
  • Relevant information

Lower similarity means:

  • Different topics
  • Unrelated concepts

Most vector databases use cosine similarity or closely related techniques to identify relevant information.

Why This Matters for RAG

Retrieval Augmented Generation depends on finding information that is semantically related to a user's question.

Suppose a user asks:

How can I learn Python?

The system converts the question into a vector. It then compares that vector against thousands of document vectors. Documents discussing:

  • Python tutorials
  • Python courses
  • Python programming guides

will have vectors close to the question vector. Those documents are retrieved and supplied to the LLM as context.

This process allows RAG systems to find relevant information even when the wording differs completely.

Key Takeaways

  • Embeddings represent meaning using vectors.
  • A vector can be viewed as a point in a high-dimensional space.
  • Similar meanings produce vectors that are close together.
  • Embedding models focus on semantic meaning rather than exact wording.
  • Word2Vec demonstrated that vector arithmetic can capture meaningful relationships between concepts.
  • Modern embedding models work with sentences, paragraphs, and entire documents.
  • Cosine similarity is commonly used to measure how close two vectors are.
  • The ability to compare meaning mathematically is the foundation of semantic search, vector databases, and RAG systems.
