Chinchilla Scaling Law

Category: AI → Models

Core Principle

For a fixed training compute budget, the best model performance comes from scaling parameters and training tokens together—not just making the model bigger.

What is the Chinchilla Scaling Law?

Introduced by DeepMind in 2022, the Chinchilla Scaling Law showed that most large language models at the time were undertrained. They had too many parameters and were trained on too little data.

The key finding: to train compute-optimally, the number of training tokens should grow in proportion to the number of parameters. A practical rule of thumb is roughly 20 training tokens per parameter.

Relationship Between Parameters and Training Tokens

  • Parameters: What the model learns and stores during training.
  • Training Tokens: The amount of text the model is trained on.
  • Balance: Both should scale together. Doubling parameters means you should roughly double the training data.

Why It Mattered

  • It corrected the "bigger is always better" assumption.
  • It proved a smaller model trained on more data can beat a larger, undertrained one.
  • Chinchilla (70B) outperformed Gopher (280B) despite being 4x smaller.
  • It made training far more compute-efficient.

Why It Matters Less Today

  • Chinchilla optimizes for training compute, not inference cost. Today inference dominates total cost.
  • Smaller models trained on far more tokens than "optimal" are cheaper to run and often preferred.
  • Data quality, curation, and new architectures now matter more than hitting an exact token ratio.

Simple Diagram

 8B Parameters   ->   X Tokens
16B Parameters   ->  2X Tokens
32B Parameters   ->  4X Tokens

As parameters scale up, training tokens should scale up proportionally.

Modern Limitations & Alternatives

Scaling parameters and data is no longer the only path to better performance. Modern techniques include:

  • Better Architectures: Mixture-of-Experts and improved attention designs.
  • Model Compression: Distillation into smaller, efficient models.
  • Quantization: Lower-precision weights for faster, cheaper inference.
  • Pruning: Removing redundant weights without losing much accuracy.
  • Inference-Time Scaling: Spending more compute at inference to improve answers.
  • RAG: Adding external knowledge instead of baking everything into parameters.
  • Reasoning Models: Thinking step-by-step before answering.

Practical Takeaway

Bigger models require more training data, but today there are many other ways to improve performance besides increasing parameters—better architectures, compression, quantization, pruning, inference-time scaling, RAG, and reasoning all play a role.
